What is Ethics?

  • Ethics is the philosophical study of morality, examining what is right and wrong, good and bad, just and unjust.
  • The basis of public policy and law.

What is Computer Ethics?

  • Based on James H. Moor (1985)
  • Introduction to the emerging field
  • Focus: key terms and conceptual insights
  • Link to paper

Computer Ethics

  • Analysis of the nature and social impact of computer technology and formulation of ethical policies
  • Commentary: Computers raise policy vacuums – new possibilities without clear rules
  • Both personal and societal policies matter

Policy Vacuum

  • Definition: A situation in which no existing policies clearly dictate what should be done with computer technology
  • Example: Protecting computer programs – what counts as "ownership"?
  • Commentary: Ethical dilemmas emerge because technology outpaces rules and norms

Conceptual Vacuum & Conceptual Muddle

  • Conceptual Vacuum: Missing definitions or categories to frame a problem
  • Conceptual Muddle: Initial clarity masks conceptual confusion
  • Example: Defining a computer program – intellectual property, process, or idea?
  • Commentary: Clarifying concepts is essential before ethical policy-making

Logical Malleability

  • Definition: Computers can be shaped to perform any activity expressed as logical operations on inputs and outputs
  • Commentary: Core revolutionary feature of computers. Enables limitless applications – the Computer Revolution
  • Example: Computers are not just calculators; they are universal tools limited only by our creativity

Permeation Stage

  • Stage where computer technology becomes integral to social institutions
  • Commentary: Questions shift from "How efficiently does it perform?" → "What is the nature of the activity?"
  • Example: Elections, education, finance – basic institutions transformed

Invisible Abuse

  • Definition: Using computer invisibility to engage in unethical acts without detection
  • Examples: Bank fraction-of-a-cent theft, unauthorized access to private data
  • Commentary: How to detect and prevent abuses that computers make invisible?

Invisible Programming Values

  • Values embedded in a program due to programmer decisions
  • Examples: Airline reservation bias (SABRE), Three Mile Island simulation choices
  • Commentary: Values can be latent or unintended; ethical policies must consider hidden biases

Invisible Complex Calculation

  • Definition: Computers perform calculations beyond human comprehension
  • Examples: Four-color conjecture (1976), military/nuclear decision systems
  • Commentary: How much can we trust invisible computation? Oversight remains essential

Integrating the Concepts

  • Computer ethics sits at the intersection of facts, concepts, policies, and values
  • Commentary: Logical malleability + invisibility = unique ethical challenges

Practical Importance

  • Computers are revolutionizing work, finance, education, and politics
  • Ethical issues will grow as technology permeates institutions
  • Commentary: Practical value lies in guiding responsible design, deployment, and use

Takeaways

  • Policy vacuums → need new ethical guidance
  • Conceptual vacuums → need clear definitions
  • Logical malleability → limitless potential, requires ethical foresight
  • Invisibility → challenge of trust and bias
  • Computer ethics is dynamic, practical, and conceptually rich
  • Commentary: Foundation for analyzing modern computing issues

Logical Arguments

  • Arguments help analyze cyberethics issues by clarifying reasoning.
  • Components:
    • Premises: One or more supporting statements
    • Conclusion: Statement that follows from the premises
  • Example scenario: Is it morally permissible to download content from a website?

Sound and Valid Arguments

  • Valid: Conclusion logically follows from premises
  • Sound: Argument is valid and all premises are true
  • All arguments are one of:
    • Valid & Unsound (untrue premises, valid logic)
    • Valid & Sound (true premises, valid logic)
    • Invalid & Inductive (generalization, logic not strictly valid)
    • Invalid & Fallacious (flawed logic).

Unsound Argument Example

  • Mice are larger than Dogs
  • Dogs are larger than Elephants
  • Conclusion: Mice are larger than Elephants

Invalid Argument Example

  • Dogs are larger than Mice
  • Elephants are larger than Dogs
  • Conclusion: Mice are larger than Elephants

Valid Argument Example

  • All websites contain code written in HTML
  • capilanou.ca is a website
  • Conclusion: capilanou.ca contains code written in HTML

Inductive Argument Example

  • 10% of Facebook is disinformation
  • My mom is a Facebook user
  • Conclusion: My mom has been exposed to disinformation

Inductive Argument Example

  • The majority of Facebook posts are disinformation
  • My mom is a Facebook user
  • Conclusion: My mom has most likely been exposed to disinformation

Weak/Inductive Example

  • Crypto investors made lots of money in the last few years
  • I will be investing in crypto
  • Conclusion: I will be making lots of money

Weak/Inductive Example

  • Twitter has a unique feature of 280 characters per post
  • Twitter is favored by journalists, celebrities, and politicians
  • Conclusion: Journalists, celebrities, and politicians enjoy writing short sentences

Moor's Argument

This is all I need to support my argument for the practical importance of computer ethics. In brief, the argument is as follows: The revolutionary feature of computers is their logical malleability. Logical malleability assures the enormous application of computer technology. This will bring about the Computer Revolution. During the Computer Revolution many of our human activities and social institutions will be transformed. These transformations will leave us with policy and conceptual vacuums about how to use computer technology. Such policy and conceptual vacuums are the marks of basic problems within computer ethics. Therefore, computer ethics is a field of substantial practical importance.
