Working in small groups, analyze a technology issue by answering the following:

  1. Identify invisible features: What aspects of the technology are hidden, overlooked, or not immediately obvious in its design or impact? Consider features that are massively complex or could enable invisible abuse.

  2. Connect to computer ethics: Why is this issue an ethical one? Consider Moor’s concepts such as logical malleability, conceptual muddle, policy vacuum, invisible programming values, or other relevant ideas.

  3. Formulate the arguments: Express both sides of the debate in logical form—state one or more premises followed by a conclusion.

  4. Evaluate the arguments: Decide which category each argument fits:

    • Valid and Unsound: Logic is valid, but one or more premises are false.

    • Valid and Sound: Logic is valid, and all premises are true.

    • Invalid and Inductive: Logic generalizes from premises that may or may not be true.

    • Invalid and Fallacious: Logic is flawed; premises may or may not be true.

For help with common fallacies, see https://yourlogicalfallacyis.com/
