Ethical Theory Core Idea / Focus Strengths Weaknesses Example
Utilitarianism (Consequentialism) Morality judged by consequences; maximize overall happiness/well-being. Outcome-focused, impartial, practical for policy. Can justify harm to few for many; outcomes can be hard to predict. Sacrificing one life to save many in a disaster.
Deontology (Duty-Based) Morality determined by rules or duties, regardless of consequences. Protects rights, respects moral principles. Can be rigid; may produce harmful outcomes if rules conflict. Never lie, even if telling the truth has bad consequences.
Virtue Ethics Focus on developing good character traits (virtues) like honesty, courage, compassion. Emphasizes moral growth, holistic view of ethics. Less clear guidance for specific dilemmas; context-dependent. A virtuous person acts honestly, regardless of rules or outcomes.
Ethical Relativism Morality depends on cultural, societal, or individual perspectives; no universal standard. Respects diversity, avoids moral imperialism. Can excuse harmful practices; difficult to criticize unethical acts. A practice moral in one culture may be immoral in another, but both are valid in context.
Rights-Based Ethics Morality centers on respecting and protecting individual rights (life, liberty, property). Strong protection for individuals; clear legal alignment. Rights can conflict; does not always resolve practical dilemmas. Free speech is protected even if controversial.
Justice / Fairness Focus on fairness, equality, and equitable distribution of benefits/burdens. Promotes equality, social fairness. Disagreement over what “fair” means (equality vs. equity vs. liberty). Progressive taxation, anti-discrimination laws.
Care Ethics Morality emphasizes relationships, empathy, and responsibility toward others, especially the vulnerable. Values compassion, context, human connection. Can seem subjective; may conflict with impartial justice. Parental leave laws, elder care, or responding to individual needs.
